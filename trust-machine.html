The playbook

How the ultra-rich pass wealth tax-free

The catalogue lists the tools. This is how they combine in the real world: first the three moves that sit under almost every large transfer, then the clearest public example we have, the structure Phil Knight built around his Nike fortune.

How the machinery works

Three moves under every billion

Strip away the acronyms and almost every sophisticated wealth-transfer plan runs the same three moves. The point is never to hide money. It is to make a fortune appear smaller than it is at the one moment the government measures it.

Move 01
Freeze

Lock today's value inside your estate using notes or annuities, so every dollar of future growth accrues to your heirs instead of to you. The asset you keep stops climbing; the one they get takes off.

Move 02
Squeeze

Shrink the reported value with minority and marketability discounts. Split one asset among several owners and you can tell the IRS the parts are worth less than the whole, even when nothing real has changed.

Move 03
Shift

Move the frozen, discounted asset into a trust that sits outside your taxable estate. Make it generation-skipping and the wealth can compound for descendants who are never taxed on it again.

The legal backing

Built by one body of law, taxed by another

The American trust sits in a gap between two legal systems that were never reconciled. Common law built the trust and, for centuries, limited it. The tax code spent a hundred years trying to tax what moves through it. Almost every technique on the other tab exists because an anti-abuse rule, written to shut one door, left another standing open.

The common-law foundation

A trust splits ownership in two: the trustee holds legal title while the beneficiary holds the beneficial interest. That division, an English equity invention refined over centuries, is what lets one person hold and manage property for another's benefit.

The common law's brake was the Rule Against Perpetuities: an interest had to vest, if at all, within a life in being plus twenty-one years. It existed to stop the dead hand from ruling property forever. Repeal that rule, as a handful of states have, and the perpetual dynasty trust becomes possible.

The federal transfer-tax trilogy

Three taxes are the thing trusts are engineered around. The estate tax arrived in 1916, the gift tax followed in 1924 and became permanent in 1932, and the generation-skipping transfer tax came in 1976.

Together they were meant to take a cut at every link in the chain: at death, at lifetime gifts, and at any attempt to leapfrog a generation. The history below is the story of how each was written, worked around, patched, and worked around again.

Why it keeps happening

Three principles under the whole game

Strip the history down and the same three forces produce loophole after loophole.

Principle 01

The mismatch

The income-tax rules and the transfer-tax rules were drafted by different hands and never aligned. You can be the owner of a trust for income tax while the same assets sit outside your estate for the estate tax. The intentionally defective grantor trust lives entirely inside that gap.

Principle 02

Federalism as a feature

Transfer tax is federal, but trust law is state. States compete to be the most permissive on trust duration, secrecy, and self-settled protection, while the federal exemption supplies the tax shield. Dynasty and asset-protection trusts live in that seam.

Principle 03

Anti-abuse rules as scaffolding

Time and again, the statute written to kill one technique becomes the authority for the next. Chapter 14 outlawed the GRIT and, in the same breath, authorized the GRAT. The grantor-trust rules meant to stop income-shifting now power the IDGT.

A note on intent: charitable trusts and special needs trusts are not loopholes. Congress built them on purpose, to encourage giving and to protect benefits for people with disabilities. The line between an incentive Congress intended and a loophole it merely tolerates is exactly what the fights below are about.

About this dispatch

This catalogue surveys the principal trust forms used in United States estate, tax, and asset-protection planning. Definitions describe how each structure functions under federal law as of 2026. Examples are drawn from public record: court decisions, SEC filings, published wills, and investigative reporting.

The headline cases (Knight, Walton, Adelson, Onassis, Helmsley) are matters of public record. Many planning structures, by design, are private, so the everyday examples describe standard practice rather than named individuals.
