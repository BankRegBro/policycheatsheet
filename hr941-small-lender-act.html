What the bill does

H.R. 941 amends the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) at Section 704B — the provision added by Section 1071 of Dodd-Frank — to make three targeted statutory changes to the CFPB's small business lending data collection framework. It does not repeal the underlying data collection requirement; it narrows who must comply and adds a mandatory compliance runway with a penalty-free grace period.

The bill responds directly to the CFPB's 2023 final rule (88 Fed. Reg. 35150, May 31, 2023), which was subject to sustained industry litigation and multiple compliance-date extensions. By writing its key thresholds into the statute rather than regulation, H.R. 941 would constrain the CFPB's regulatory discretion on coverage and timing.

Legislative context

H.R. 941 is one of two 119th Congress House bills targeting Section 1071. The related (but distinct) bill, H.R. 976 (1071 Repeal to Protect Small Business Lending Act), would repeal ECOA §704B entirely; its Senate counterpart is S. 557. The Small LENDER Act takes the narrower approach of preserving the data collection mandate while adjusting its scope and providing compliance relief. Separately, the CFPB pursued the regulatory route: it proposed a narrowed "reconsideration" rule in November 2025 and finalized it on May 1, 2026, independently adopting many of the same coverage and definitional changes the bill would write into statute.

Statutory target

The bill amends Section 704B of the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (15 U.S.C. 1691c–2), which was added to ECOA by Section 1071 of the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (2010). The CFPB implements this authority through Regulation B, Subpart B (12 C.F.R. §1002).

View:
[No comparable provision exists in current ECOA §704B(g). The subsection grants the CFPB general rulemaking authority to "prescribe such rules and issue such guidance as may be necessary to carry out, enforce, and compile data pursuant to this section." No statutory compliance timeline or safe harbor is specified — those terms are set entirely by CFPB rulemaking.]
"(4) COMPLIANCE WITH COVERED RULE.— "(A) IN GENERAL.—With respect to the covered rule, the Bureau shall provide a financial institution a 3-year period beginning on the date the covered rule was issued to comply with the rule. "(B) SAFE HARBOR.—After the end of the 3-year period described under subparagraph (A), the Bureau shall provide a 2-year safe harbor to a financial institution during which the financial institution is required to comply with the covered rule but is not subject to any penalties for failure to comply with the covered rule. "(C) COVERED RULE DEFINED.—In this paragraph, the term 'covered rule' means the final rule of the Bureau titled 'Small Business Lending Under the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (Regulation B)' (88 Fed. Reg. 35150, published May 31, 2023)."
Effect: Codifies a 5-year total runway (3-year compliance + 2-year no-penalty) running from the covered rule's issuance, placing the hard compliance start at approximately May 31, 2026 and extending penalty-free status through roughly May 31, 2028. The CFPB could not shorten this timeline without a statutory amendment. Two caveats: "covered rule" is defined as the 2023 final rule specifically, so these clocks would not attach to the CFPB's 2026 final rule; and "the date the covered rule was issued" is arguably ambiguous — the rule was issued March 30, 2023 but published May 31, 2023.
ECOA §704B(h)(1)
Definition of "financial institution"
Raises and codifies the loan origination threshold triggering data collection obligations
View:
"(1) FINANCIAL INSTITUTION.—The term 'financial institution' means any partnership, company, corporation, association (incorporated or unincorporated), trust, estate, cooperative organization, or other entity that engages in any financial activity." [Note: The current statute specifies NO numeric origination threshold. Congress delegated coverage to the CFPB; the 2023 final rule set it at 100 originations in each of the 2 preceding calendar years. The threshold is a regulatory construct, not statutory text.]
"(1) FINANCIAL INSTITUTION.—The term 'financial institution' means— "(A) any partnership, company, corporation, association (incorporated or unincorporated), trust, estate, cooperative organization, or other entity that engages in any financial activity; and "(B) in each of the previous 2 calendar years originated not less than 500 credit transactions for small businesses."
Effect: The bill strikes the existing one-sentence definition and inserts a two-part test adding a 500-transaction statutory floor (in each of the previous 2 calendar years). Because the current statute sets no threshold, the bill is establishing one in law for the first time — not "raising" a statutory figure. The CFPB could set a higher threshold by rule (the 2026 final rule uses 1,000) but could not go below 500 without a statutory change. Lenders below 500 small-business originations annually would be exempt as a matter of law.
ECOA §704B(h)(2)
Definition of "small business"
Redefines "small business" by codifying a $1M gross annual revenue ceiling in the statute
View:
"(2) SMALL BUSINESS.—The term 'small business' has the same meaning as the term 'small business concern' in section 3 of the Small Business Act (15 U.S.C. 632). [Note: The 2023 CFPB rule set the threshold at $5,000,000 in gross annual revenues, departing from the SBA's industry-specific size standards in favor of a uniform revenue cap.]
"(2) SMALL BUSINESS.—The term 'small business' means any entity with gross annual revenues of $1,000,000 or less in the most recently completed fiscal year."
Effect: Replaces the SBA cross-reference (and the CFPB's $5M regulatory threshold) with a flat $1M revenue cap written into the statute. Businesses above $1M in revenues fall outside the data collection regime as a matter of law — the CFPB could not expand coverage to larger businesses without congressional action. The CFPB's 2026 final rule independently adopted the same $1M figure, but because it is regulatory it remains revisable by a future administration; the bill would entrench it.
1
Mandatory compliance runway — 3 years from rule issuance
The bill requires the CFPB to provide every covered financial institution a full 3 years from the date the 2023 final rule was issued (read here as May 31, 2023) before requiring compliance. This is a statutory floor — the Bureau cannot shorten it. Running from the rule's publication, the compliance window would extend to approximately May 31, 2026. This codification prevents the CFPB from compressing the timeline in future rulemakings or reversing prior deadline extensions. Note a wrinkle introduced by intervening events: the rule's compliance dates have since been extended to July 2026–October 2027 (2023 rule, as amended) and January 1, 2028 (2026 final rule) — all later than the bill's ~May 2026 date. As drafted against the 2023 rule, the bill could therefore accelerate compliance relative to the current regulatory calendar rather than relax it.
2
Two-year penalty-free safe harbor after compliance begins
After the 3-year compliance period, the bill provides a 2-year safe harbor during which institutions must technically comply with the rule but face no penalties for failure to do so. This effectively creates a 5-year total ramp-up period (through approximately May 2028) before enforcement exposure arises. The safe harbor is universal — it applies to all covered institutions, not only smaller lenders.
3
Coverage threshold: 500 originations per year (statutory floor)
The bill writes a 500-transaction origination threshold directly into ECOA. Under the 2023 CFPB rule, coverage began at 100 originations (per year, for 2 consecutive years). By placing 500 in the statute, the bill makes that floor a congressional mandate. Lenders below 500 small business originations annually are exempt as a matter of law — the CFPB cannot by rule expand coverage downward without congressional action. The CFPB retains discretion to set a higher threshold by rule.
4
Small business defined as ≤ $1M gross annual revenues
The bill eliminates the current SBA cross-reference definition and replaces it with a flat $1 million gross annual revenue ceiling, measured in the most recently completed fiscal year. The CFPB's 2023 rule set the cap at $5M; the CFPB's 2026 final rule (published May 1, 2026) lowered it to $1M by regulation. The bill would codify this $1M figure in the statute, meaning only loans to entities with $1M or less in annual revenues trigger collection obligations — removing a substantial segment of mid-market commercial lending from coverage. The practical difference now is durability: the regulatory $1M figure can be revised in a future rulemaking, while a statutory figure cannot.
5
Scope is limited to the 2023 final rule
The compliance timeline provisions (Provisions 1 and 2) are specifically tied to the CFPB's 2023 final rule (88 Fed. Reg. 35150) by name and Federal Register citation. They do not automatically extend to any replacement or revised rule the CFPB might issue. This is no longer hypothetical: the CFPB's 2026 final rule (published May 1, 2026) substantially revises and supersedes the 2023 rule's architecture. As drafted, the bill's clocks would attach to a rule the agency has now largely rewritten — so if H.R. 941 advances, the "covered rule" citation likely needs updating to the 2026 rule (or to be made rule-agnostic). The definitions in Provisions 3 and 4, by contrast, are written into ECOA §704B(h) generally and would apply to any future rule implementing Section 1071.

Key analytical distinctions

Bill vs. CFPB 2026 final rule — overlapping outcomes, different legal durability. The CFPB's 2026 final rule and H.R. 941 converge on the same $1M small-business revenue threshold and an exemption for the smallest lenders. The difference is durability, not direction: the rule's $1M figure was set by regulation and can be amended or reversed through future notice-and-comment rulemaking — the rule has already been revised twice since 2023. H.R. 941 would entrench the figure in statute, insulating it from regulatory revision. That is the value the legislative route adds now that the regulatory outcome largely matches the bill.

Origination thresholds diverge. The bill sets 500 as a statutory floor; the CFPB's 2026 final rule set the regulatory threshold at 1,000 (the preamble notes the Bureau also considered 200, 500, and 2,000). If H.R. 941 were enacted alongside the current rule, the higher 1,000-transaction threshold would govern in practice — but the Bureau could never lower it below 500 without legislation.

The bill narrows more surgically than H.R. 976. The related House bill (H.R. 976; Senate counterpart S. 557) would repeal ECOA §704B entirely, eliminating the CFPB's data collection authority. H.R. 941 leaves the underlying mandate intact but constrains its scope and timeline — a more defensible position against fair lending objections and, in principle, more likely to survive Senate scrutiny.

Compliance timeline provisions are rule-specific — and the superseding rule now exists. The 3-year compliance period and 2-year safe harbor are tied by citation to the 2023 final rule only. With the CFPB's 2026 final rule now published (May 1, 2026) and substantially revising the 2023 framework, the statutory clock would not restart automatically and arguably would attach to a rule the agency has rewritten. If H.R. 941 advances, this is the most likely drafting fix — repointing "covered rule" to the 2026 rule or making it rule-agnostic.
