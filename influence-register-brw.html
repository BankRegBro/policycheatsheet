The trade groups that work the financial perimeter

An interactive field guide to the banking, mortgage, crypto, and fintech associations that draft the comment letters, fund the PACs, and file the lawsuits shaping policy in Washington. Sorted by pillar, cross-tagged by the rivalries that move the debate.

Banking Mortgage Crypto Fintech

Washington's financial-policy debates are easier to read as conflicts than as a roster. A capital rule, an open-banking mandate, a stablecoin bill: each pits a coalition against a rival, and the same handful of associations show up on opposite sides again and again. This tool profiles the heavyweights in four pillars, then cross-tags each to the six fault lines where the real fights happen.

Method & caveats

How this register was built, what is verified, and what to treat with caution.

What this is

A structured reference on the heavyweight financial-services trade associations operating in Washington across four pillars: banking, mortgage, crypto and digital assets, and fintech, plus the cross-cutting groups that touch all of them. Each entry carries founding, headquarters, mission, membership, current leadership, signature positions, lobbying and PAC activity, named campaigns, coalitions, and the tensions most relevant to an outside stakeholder.

Sourcing

Built from primary sources where possible: association websites, IRS Form 990 filings, Senate LDA lobbying disclosures, FEC and OpenSecrets data, and reputable trade press. Lobbying figures are 2024 annual totals from OpenSecrets unless noted. Membership counts and asset-coverage figures are largely association-supplied and should be read as promotional unless tied to a regulator source or a 990.

Volatility warnings

  • Crypto leadership turned over heavily in 2025. Blockchain Association, Crypto Council for Innovation, The Digital Chamber, and the DeFi Education Fund all changed top leadership that year. Re-verify before any publication-critical use.
  • AFSA (Winslow replaced Himpler, July 2025) and ALTA (Morton replaced Tomb, June 2025) also changed chief executives recently. Older sources still list predecessors.
  • The CFPB Section 1033 open-banking rule is in flux. A November 2025 preliminary injunction blocks enforcement while the Bureau pursues a new rulemaking. The rule has not been finally vacated.
  • Some 2024 lobbying figures were not separately retrieved (BPI, CBA, CCI, and several smaller groups). Pull these from Senate LDA before relying on them.

Classification notes

Coin Center is a research and advocacy nonprofit closer to a think tank than a member-funded trade group. Stand With Crypto is a grassroots mobilization vehicle, and Fairshake is a super PAC network rather than a trade association. All are flagged where they appear because they shape the same debates from a different posture.

About this dispatch

The Influence Register is a BankRegWire field tool for the Washington policy community: members and staff, agency officials, journalists, and the associations themselves. It is descriptive, not an endorsement of any group's positions, and the policy characterizations are drawn from each association's own public advocacy.

Read the perimeter by its rivalries, not its rosters.
