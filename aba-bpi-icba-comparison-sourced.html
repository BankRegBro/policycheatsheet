Methodology & caveats

This is now sourced. Each cell\u2019s stance and ask are drawn from the named association\u2019s own comment letters, testimony, press statements, and issue pages, dated 2024\u20132026 and linked in the issue detail. Short quotations are reproduced sparingly; everything else is paraphrased. The comparison is issue-centric — each row is one policy question, three trade voices read across it, ordered largest to smallest (BPI \u00b7 ABA \u00b7 ICBA).

The alignment verdict is an editorial reading of where the three land, typically anchored by the BPI\u2194ICBA poles. A joint flag is the hard signal: it marks issues where a co-signed filing exists among the named trades (the flag lists who). Note that \u201cjoint\u201d can mean two of the three — ABA + BPI on Basel, BPI + ICBA on ILCs — not always all three.

The finding worth stating plainly: across these six issues there is no pure Opposed row. In the current deregulatory cycle the three align more than the small-versus-large narrative predicts — they even converge against outside threats (the credit-card routing mandate; commercial firms entering via ILCs). The real divergences are by exposure, not head-to-head conflict: Basel splits the parties because community banks sit outside a $100B+ rule; mergers split them structurally even though all three backed the 2025 FDIC rollback. The sharper opposition the fault-line framing anticipates lives in issues not yet in this set — deposit-concentration caps, too-big-to-fail surcharges, the Durbin small-issuer exemption — natural Phase-2 additions.

Four of eighteen cells are directional: orientation established from the public record, specific primary source not retrieved (BPI on credit-union tax and §1071; ABA on ILCs; ICBA on Basel). Several underlying rules have since moved — the Basel reproposal went capital-neutral (Mar 2026) and the §1071 rule was finalized in narrowed form (May 2026).
