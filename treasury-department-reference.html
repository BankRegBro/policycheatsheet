The U.S. Treasury.

The federal government's finance department, standing since 1789. It collects the revenue, manages the debt, manufactures the cash, supervises the national banks, and runs the sanctions. Almost everything it does happens inside nine operating bureaus, the IRS chief among them.

Established · 2 Sept 1789 Codified · Title 31 U.S.C. Secretary · Bessent (79th) Staff · ≈114,000 Bureaus · 9 operating
I

What it does

Functions · Bureaus · Scale

Treasury is the government's money plumbing. It brings the revenue in, sends the payments out, finances the gap by selling debt, and prints and mints the currency itself. The policy work sits in the Departmental Offices; the operations sit in the bureaus, which hold about 98 percent of the workforce.

  • 01Collects the revenue. Taxes, customs duties, and monies due to the United States, chiefly through the IRS.
  • 02Pays the bills. Disburses nearly all federal payments and keeps the government's accounts.
  • 03Manages the public debt. Issues Treasury bills, notes, and bonds and services the outstanding debt.
  • 04Makes the money. Mints coin and prints paper currency and federal securities.
  • 05Supervises national banks. Charters and examines national banks and federal thrifts through the OCC.
  • 06Advises on policy. Domestic and international financial, monetary, economic, trade, and fiscal policy.
  • 07Enforces the finance laws. Pursues tax evaders, counterfeiters, and forgers.
  • 08Runs the sanctions. Administers economic and trade sanctions through OFAC.
  • 09Fights financial crime. Administers the Bank Secrecy Act and the AML/CFT regime through FinCEN.

Total outlays · FY2024
≈ $1.3 T
About a fifth of all federal spending, third among agencies. But this is overwhelmingly interest on the public debt and refundable tax credits, not the cost of running the place.
Operating budget
≈ $13–14 B
The discretionary cost of the department itself. The IRS is by far the largest piece, collecting about $4.7 trillion, near 96 percent of the funding that runs the federal government.
II

Who leads it

As of 2026 · time-sensitive

Scott Bessent, the 79th Secretary of the Treasury, sworn in 28 January 2025 on a 68 to 29 Senate vote. The Secretary is the government's chief financial officer and the President's principal economic advisor, and sits fifth in the line of presidential succession.

AppointmentNominated by the President, confirmed by the Senate after a Finance Committee hearing, under the Appointments Clause. A Level I Executive Schedule post.
In the CabinetA member of the Cabinet by custom and of the National Security Council by law.
SuccessionFifth in the presidential line of succession, after the Secretary of State and before the Secretary of Defense.
On the currencyThe signatures of the Secretary and the Treasurer of the United States both appear on Federal Reserve notes.
III

Where its authority comes from

Constitution · Title 31 · Statute

The power of the purse. Treasury executes the taxing, borrowing, and coinage powers that Article I of the Constitution gives to Congress, plus the spending that only an appropriation can release. Its organization is codified in Title 31 of the U.S. Code, and individual functions trace to their own statutes.

Art. I · §8 · cl. 1
Tax & Spend
Congress may lay and collect taxes, duties, imposts, and excises.
Art. I · §8 · cl. 2
Borrow
Borrow money on the credit of the United States.
Art. I · §8 · cl. 5
Coin Money
Coin money and regulate its value.
Art. I · §9 · cl. 7
Appropriations
No money drawn from the Treasury but by appropriation made in law.
Art. I · §8 · cl. 18
Necessary & Proper
Hamilton's implied-powers basis for the First Bank, later affirmed in McCulloch v. Maryland.
31 U.S.C. §301
The Department
Establishes Treasury as an executive department and the Secretary's general authority at §321.

The founding statute is the Act of 2 September 1789, which created the Department and the offices of Secretary, Comptroller, Auditor, Treasurer, and Register. Alexander Hamilton was sworn in nine days later. Each major function below carries its own delegated authority. Pick one.

IV

Major events

1775 → 2025 · tap to expand

Treasury is the second-oldest department in the government, and most of its present shape was set in three bursts: the founding under Hamilton, the financing of the Civil War, and the modern fiscal state built around 1913. Filter the spine, or open any entry.

Reading notes

  • Leadership is volatile. As of mid-2026 there is no Senate-confirmed Deputy Secretary or IRS Commissioner; both are filled on a "performing the duties" basis. Re-verify the officeholder fields against treasury.gov before relying on them.
  • The headline spending figure is not operating cost. Treasury's roughly $1.3 trillion in outlays is mostly interest on the public debt and refundable tax credits. Net interest on the debt passed $1 trillion for the first time in FY2025.
  • Staff counts diverge by measure. The ≈114,000 department-wide figure is a September 2024 OPM number; 2025 workforce reductions lowered it. The IRS alone has multiple official measures (about 90,500 FTE; over 100,000 headcount) that should not be conflated.
  • Some bodies evolved in stages. FinCEN began as a 1990 Treasury order and became a statutory bureau in 2002; the Mint was founded in 1792 but only came fully under Treasury in 1873. Both dates appear on the spine.
